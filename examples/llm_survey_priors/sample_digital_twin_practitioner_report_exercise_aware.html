What This Report Means by Digital Twins

In this report, a digital twin is an AI simulation of an individual survey respondent: the model sees some of that respondent's prior survey answers, then predicts how the same respondent would answer a held-out or new question. One practical analogy is persona-based reasoning in product design and user research: practitioners have long used personas to make abstract customer data easier to reason with and to ask "what would this kind of person need, object to, or choose?" Digital twins are not the same as traditional personas, but they extend a related idea: they use many respondent-specific records, apply the persona-like reasoning at scale, and ask a model to project each respondent into a new decision setting.

This approach builds on a growing literature using language models to simulate human samples and behavior, including Argyle et al.'s work on "silicon samples" and algorithmic fidelity, Aher, Arriaga, and Kalai's "Turing Experiments" for replicating human-subject studies, Horton's framing of LLMs as simulated economic agents, and Park et al.'s generative-agent simulations of 1,000 real people. Expected Parrot is the company and open-source tooling provider behind EDSL, the system used here to build surveys, run language-model jobs remotely, store results, and generate this report.

References: Cooper, The Inmates Are Running the Asylum; Pruitt &amp; Grudin, Personas; Argyle et al., Out of One, Many; Aher, Arriaga &amp; Kalai, Using Large Language Models to Simulate Multiple Humans; Horton, Large Language Models as Simulated Economic Agents; Park et al., Generative Agent Simulations of 1,000 People; Expected Parrot EDSL documentation.

Why This Report Uses Held-Out Questions

The held-out question is a proxy for the kind of new question a practitioner might later ask an instantiated digital twin. In a deployed use case, the model would see what is already known about a respondent and then project that respondent into a new decision setting. The benchmark recreates that situation by hiding one real survey question, giving the twin other answers from the same respondent, and scoring whether the twin can recover the answer the respondent actually gave.

This is intentionally a demanding test. Survey designers usually avoid asking many highly correlated questions when they can, because redundant questions waste respondent attention. That means a held-out item is often not just a near-duplicate of the context questions. If a twin performs well under that condition, it is evidence that it learned something useful about the respondent or question family, not merely that it copied an obvious neighboring item.

The hold-out design is still only a proxy. It tests generalization to questions from the same survey environment, with known response options and a real answer for scoring. A truly new question may use different wording, a different scale, or a different decision context. Treat strong hold-out performance as evidence of promise, then adjust confidence based on how similar the new question is to the tested held-out questions.

How to Use This Report

Match Evidence to the Intended Use

Do not read one benchmark as a single yes/no answer about whether "digital twins work." A report may contain several twin exercises: different surveys, question families, held-out items, respondent samples, and models. Treat each exercise as evidence about a particular kind of use.

The standard of evidence depends less on whether the decision sounds "low" or "high" stakes in the abstract, and more on what the user needs the twin output to do:

  • Exact levels or public quantitative claims: require the strongest evidence. If the goal is to say "62% would choose A," publish a population estimate, set a hard cutoff, or target people based on predicted probabilities, inspect calibration and run stronger validation.
  • Rank ordering or prioritization: can tolerate more error in levels. If the goal is to decide which message, option, segment, or question is more promising, the key question is whether the benchmark shows reliable ordering, not whether every percentage is right.
  • Exploration and surfacing considerations: can be useful even when point estimates are uncertain. If the goal is to find likely objections, missing arguments, subgroup concerns, or hypotheses to investigate, the twin output should be judged as structured qualitative evidence rather than as a survey estimate.

Decision stakes still matter: the more public, irreversible, costly, or consequential the decision, the more direct validation is warranted. But a fast internal ranking exercise and a publishable estimate should not be held to the same standard.

Read Performance by Exercise

When a benchmark spans multiple surveys or held-out question families, discuss the results separately. A binary climate-policy exercise, a multi-option skill-importance exercise, a social-media attitude exercise, and a vignette blame exercise are not the same test. Strong performance in one exercise should not be generalized to all twins, and weak performance in one exercise should not erase evidence from another. Look for the pattern: which question types, option structures, and context signals worked, and which did not.

When Direct Survey Research Is Infeasible

Sometimes survey research is infeasible rather than merely expensive. Competitors, regulators, voters in a future election, executives, or other strategically important audiences may not answer a survey at all, may not answer quickly enough, or may not answer candidly. In those cases, the right comparison is not always "twin output versus a perfect survey"; it may be "twin output versus no direct measurement." Twins can be useful in that setting even when they are not precise enough to publish as population estimates.

Use Twins for Ranking and Exploration

Distinguish rank ordering from exact levels. Twins may be more reliable at identifying which option, message, segment, or question is relatively stronger than at estimating the exact percentage that would choose it.

They can also surface considerations, objections, and patterns to investigate, not just point-estimate predictions. For example, a twin study might suggest that a proposal is likely to worry respondents less because of its cost than because it feels unfair to a subgroup, or it might reveal an objection the research team had not thought to test directly. Treat those outputs as hypotheses and prompts for better thinking, not just as percentages.

1. Executive summary

Use the benchmark as five separate exercises, not as one blanket verdict. The climate-policy exercise is the only one with a large enough held-out test to support direct quantitative use for similar questions. The other four exercises are informative, but each rests on only 20 tested respondents per model and should be treated as directional unless the decision is low-stakes or direct measurement is infeasible.

What you can do now:

  • Climate-policy favor/oppose questions: Use the openai:gpt-5.5 climate-policy twin as a credible input for low- and medium-stakes decisions about similar binary climate-policy proposals. It was correct on 83.8% of 600 held-out respondent-question cases and beat both random guessing and guessing from the group average. It is especially useful for distinguishing respondent-specific support on more contested proposals such as taxing corporations for carbon emissions and requiring power plants to eliminate emissions by 2040.

Main caveat: it still missed some rare “Oppose” respondents with very high confidence, especially on broadly popular proposals such as planting a trillion trees, methane-leak sealing, and home energy-efficiency tax credits. Do not use it uncritically to identify opponents, claim near-unanimous support, or make probability-threshold decisions without inspecting those misses.

  • Skill-importance question: Treat the interpersonal-skills exercise as a promising small-sample sanity check, not a full validation of future skill-prioritization surveys. Both models beat random guessing and the group-average baseline on top-choice accuracy, but the test covered only one held-out item and 20 respondents. openai:gpt-5.5 is safer if you need probability estimates. google:gemini-2.5-pro had slightly higher top-choice accuracy but assigned effectively zero probability to the real answer in two misses, making its confidence values unsafe.
  • AI-versus-human medical diagnosis question: Use the openai:gpt-5.5 result for directional exploration of similar “AI would do better/worse/about the same/not sure” questions. It beat random guessing and group-average guessing, but the 20-person test is too small for exact public estimates. google:gemini-2.5-pro was more overconfident and should not be used for probability cutoffs here.
  • Social-media statement rating: Use the social-media exercise mainly for broad direction or prioritization, especially if you can collapse adjacent ordered categories. openai:gpt-5.5 beat both baselines; google:gemini-2.5-pro also beat the group average on confidence-quality measures but was more overconfident. The main uncertainty is distinguishing “Very well” from “Somewhat well” and detecting rare negative responses.
  • Vignette blame-attribution question: Do not use the recorded vignette exercise for quantitative decisions. The question used a seven-point agreement scale, the available context contained summarized vignette labels rather than full vignette prose, and both models performed worse than the group-average baseline on confidence quality. google:gemini-2.5-pro had several extremely confident wrong answers, including assigning zero probability to the real answer. This exercise is useful mainly as a warning about using twins on vignette outcomes without full stimulus text and stronger validation.

Model choice across exercises: openai:gpt-5.5 was the more dependable model for practitioner use in this benchmark. It was not always the highest top-choice accuracy model, but its confidence was usually better behaved. google:gemini-2.5-pro sometimes picked the right answer often enough, but its probability outputs were much less reliable in the skill, AI, social-media, and vignette exercises.

Strongest practical warning: If your use case depends on confidence values — rankings, thresholds, segment targeting, “only act when probability exceeds X,” or uncertainty bands — inspect the overconfident misses before acting. The benchmark contains several cases where a model was very confident and flat wrong.

Models tested

Each exercise tested:

  • openai:gpt-5.5
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro

Baselines used in the benchmark

The benchmark compares each model with two practical reference points:

  • Random guessing: choosing among the available response options with no respondent information. For example, random guessing is 50% on a two-option favor/oppose question, 25% on a four-option question, 20% on a five-option question, and about 14% on a seven-option question.
  • Group-average guessing: predicting from how the whole tested sample answered the held-out question. Beating this baseline means the twin used respondent-specific information beyond the overall distribution of answers. This baseline is useful for interpreting held-out benchmark questions, but it is not available for a genuinely new question before anyone has answered it.

Quality checks and caveats

  • All recorded jobs show 0 import or extraction issues.
  • Held-out response options are available as human-readable labels, not just numeric codes.
  • The recorded artifacts do not state that the evaluation results are weighted. Treat these benchmark scores as respondent-level prediction tests unless survey weights are explicitly applied in a later analysis.
  • Four exercises used only 20 respondents per model. Their results are useful as early evidence, but single-case swings can materially change the percentages.
  • The climate-policy exercise is much larger and more convincing than the other four: 600 held-out cases per model across six related binary questions.

3. Overall performance

The practical implication is clear: use the climate-policy results differently from the small exploratory exercises. The climate-policy exercise gives credible evidence for similar binary policy questions; the other exercises are mostly directional until expanded.

Here, accuracy means how often the model’s highest-probability answer matched the real respondent answer. I also refer to the average probability assigned to the real answer; higher is better. When discussing confidence quality, I use plain language: a model is penalized heavily when it gives very little probability to the answer the respondent actually chose.

Climate-policy exercise: strong with openai:gpt-5.5, mixed with google:gemini-2.5-pro

For the six binary climate-policy proposals, openai:gpt-5.5 is strong enough to support practical use on similar questions.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 83.8% of the time across 600 respondent-question cases.
  • Random guessing would be 50%.
  • Group-average guessing was correct 79.5% of the time.
  • openai:gpt-5.5 assigned an average probability of 79.1% to the real answer, compared with 68.8% for the group-average baseline.
  • Its confidence was also reasonably well behaved overall, though not perfect.

The model’s advantage over group-average guessing was not uniform across proposals. It was most valuable on the more contested or ideologically informative policies:

  • Taxing corporations based on the amount of carbon emissions they produce: openai:gpt-5.5 accuracy was 79%, compared with 70% for group-average guessing.
  • Requiring power plants to eliminate all carbon emissions by 2040: openai:gpt-5.5 accuracy was 79%, compared with 62% for group-average guessing.
  • Providing a tax credit to encourage businesses to develop technology which captures and stores carbon emissions: openai:gpt-5.5 accuracy was 85%, compared with 81% for group-average guessing.
  • Providing a tax credit to Americans who improve their home energy efficiency: openai:gpt-5.5 accuracy was 88%, compared with 86% for group-average guessing.

On highly popular proposals, group-average guessing was already very strong. For planting about a trillion trees, group-average guessing was correct 91% of the time, while openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 89%. That does not mean the twin failed entirely, but it means the top-answer accuracy was driven heavily by the proposal’s broad popularity.

google:gemini-2.5-pro was useful but less convincing in the climate-policy exercise.

  • It was correct 78.8% of the time, slightly below the 79.5% group-average accuracy.
  • It beat random guessing, but it did not reliably beat the group-average baseline overall.
  • It did add value on some contested policies, especially the power-plant requirement and corporate carbon tax, but it was weaker on the more broadly popular proposals.

Practical read: For similar binary climate-policy favor/oppose questions, prefer openai:gpt-5.5. Use google:gemini-2.5-pro as a secondary model check rather than as the primary source of probabilities.

Skill-importance exercise: promising, but small and confidence-sensitive

For the interpersonal-skills importance question, both models beat random guessing and group-average guessing on top-choice accuracy, but the sample is only 20 respondents.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 65% of the time.
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro was correct 70% of the time.
  • Random guessing among five options would be 20%.
  • Group-average guessing was correct 45% of the time.

The top-choice result looks good, but the confidence story differs by model.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 assigned an average probability of 47.8% to the real answer and beat the group-average baseline on confidence quality.
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro assigned a higher average probability to the real answer, 60.9%, and had higher top-choice accuracy, but it also made catastrophic probability errors: in two cases, it assigned effectively 0% probability to the respondent’s actual answer.

Practical read: This is useful evidence that twins can capture broad importance judgments on a familiar ordered scale, but it is not enough to estimate exact shares across all five categories. Prefer openai:gpt-5.5 if confidence values matter.

AI-versus-human medical diagnosis exercise: useful directionally, not exact

For the AI medical-diagnosis question, both models beat random guessing and group-average guessing, with openai:gpt-5.5 clearly safer.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 60% of the time.
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro was correct 50% of the time.
  • Random guessing among four options would be 25%.
  • Group-average guessing was correct 30% of the time.

openai:gpt-5.5 also had better confidence behavior. google:gemini-2.5-pro had an average top confidence of 77.9% but was correct only 50% of the time, which is too overconfident for threshold-based decisions.

Practical read: Use the openai:gpt-5.5 result for directional exploration of similar AI-vs-human judgments. Do not use this 20-person test to make exact public claims about national opinion.

Social-media statement exercise: useful for broad direction, weaker for category edges

For the social-media statement “Helps give a voice to underrepresented groups,” both models beat random guessing and group-average guessing, but the small sample and ordered response categories limit precision.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 60% of the time.
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro was correct 55% of the time.
  • Random guessing among four options would be 25%.
  • Group-average guessing was correct 50% of the time.

openai:gpt-5.5 had better overall accuracy and more moderate confidence. google:gemini-2.5-pro assigned a higher average probability to the real answer, but its confidence was less well calibrated.

Practical read: This exercise supports broad directional use, especially if the decision can tolerate “Very well” and “Somewhat well” being close substitutes. It is weaker for exact category-level estimates.

Vignette blame-attribution exercise: not decision-ready

The vignette exercise performed poorly for quantitative use.

  • openai:gpt-5.5 was correct 30% of the time, the same as group-average guessing.
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro was correct 40% of the time, but its confidence quality was very poor.
  • Random guessing among seven options would be about 14%.
  • Group-average guessing assigned more probability to the real answer than openai:gpt-5.5 did and was much safer than google:gemini-2.5-pro.

The most important issue is not simply low accuracy. The bigger problem is that both models, especially google:gemini-2.5-pro, sometimes placed very little or no probability on the real answer. In a seven-point blame scale, those mistakes can be extreme: one recorded google:gemini-2.5-pro miss predicted “Strongly disagree” when the actual answer was “Strongly agree” and assigned the actual answer effectively 0% probability.

Practical read: Do not use this recorded vignette exercise for exact estimates, thresholds, or treatment-effect decisions. Re-run with full vignette text and a larger validation sample before relying on it.

4. Where the twins worked well

The strongest evidence is for binary climate-policy questions with familiar issue structure

The climate-policy exercise worked best because the questions had clear option semantics — Favor versus Oppose — and likely linked strongly to stable respondent characteristics and prior policy attitudes.

openai:gpt-5.5 was especially useful on the climate proposals where group-average guessing left more room for respondent-level information:

  • Taxing corporations based on the amount of carbon emissions they produce: the model beat the group-average baseline by a meaningful margin. This is the type of proposal where respondent ideology, views of business, climate concern, and attitudes toward taxation plausibly provide useful signal.
  • Requiring power plants to eliminate all carbon emissions by 2040: the model substantially beat group-average guessing. This proposal is more regulatory and potentially more polarizing, so respondent-specific context mattered.
  • Providing a tax credit to encourage businesses to develop carbon-capture technology: the model beat group-average guessing and random guessing; the policy combines climate action with business incentives, which may allow context to distinguish supporters and opponents.
  • Providing a home energy-efficiency tax credit: the model performed well overall, though broad support means group-average guessing was also strong.

Use implication: For internal decision-making about similar binary policy proposals — for example, deciding which climate proposal to test further, which message to prioritize, or which respondent segments may be more supportive — the openai:gpt-5.5 climate-policy result is strong enough to act on when the decision is low- or medium-stakes. For expensive public campaigns, policy claims, or publication-quality estimates, validate with a weighted survey or a larger hold-out study.

The skill-importance exercise captured the broad importance of interpersonal skills

On the interpersonal-skills item, both models usually distinguished high-importance responses from lower-importance responses. Most errors were close to the dominant high-importance categories, especially “Extremely important” versus “Very important.”

For openai:gpt-5.5:

  • Actual “Extremely important” responses were predicted as “Extremely important” in 8 of 9 cases.
  • Actual “Very important” responses were split between “Very important” and “Extremely important.”

This is useful if the practitioner’s question is broad, such as whether interpersonal skills are generally seen as important. It is less useful if the decision depends on the exact split between “Extremely” and “Very” important.

Use implication: Good for low-stakes prioritization and initial exploration of broad importance. Not yet enough for exact category shares or for generalizing to a larger list of skills.

The AI medical-diagnosis exercise showed respondent-specific signal beyond the group average

The AI medical-diagnosis question had four substantively different options: better, worse, about the same, and not sure. openai:gpt-5.5 improved substantially over group-average guessing:

  • It was correct 60% of the time versus 30% for group-average guessing.
  • It assigned much more probability to the real answer than group-average guessing did.

The confusion pattern suggests the model could capture some real distinction between respondents who think AI would do better and those who think AI would do worse:

  • Actual “AI would do this worse”: openai:gpt-5.5 predicted “worse” in 4 of 6 cases.
  • Actual “AI would do this better”: it predicted “better” in 4 of 6 cases.

Use implication: Useful for exploratory segmentation or message testing on similar AI-comparison questions, especially when direct measurement is too slow. Still too small for exact levels.

The social-media exercise was useful for broad positive versus negative sentiment

On “Social media... Helps give a voice to underrepresented groups,” openai:gpt-5.5 performed best and usually kept errors near adjacent categories.

Examples from the confusion pattern:

  • Actual “Somewhat well” responses were predicted correctly in 7 of 10 cases.
  • Actual “Very well” responses were split between “Very well” and “Somewhat well.”
  • Actual “Not at all well” and “Not too well” were harder, but the model did not simply collapse everything into the most positive option.

Use implication: Useful for directional reads on broad social-media attitudes. If the decision is whether a statement is broadly resonant, this exercise is encouraging. If the decision depends on exact intensity, validate further.

5. Where the twins failed

Climate-policy twins overpredicted support for some rare opponents

The climate-policy exercise’s headline accuracy is strong, but some failures matter for practice. On popular climate proposals, openai:gpt-5.5 often predicted “Favor” for people who actually answered “Oppose,” sometimes with very high confidence.

Examples from the recorded misses:

  • On planting about a trillion trees, several actual “Oppose” respondents were predicted as “Favor” with only 1%–2% probability assigned to the real answer.
  • On home energy-efficiency tax credits, actual “Oppose” respondents were predicted as “Favor” with only 2% probability assigned to the real answer.
  • On requiring oil and gas companies to seal methane gas leaks, an actual “Oppose” respondent was predicted as “Favor” with only 3% probability assigned to the real answer.

The confusion table shows the same pattern. For planting about a trillion trees, openai:gpt-5.5 correctly identified 89 of 91 actual “Favor” respondents, but it did not correctly identify any of the 9 actual “Oppose” respondents. That is a major warning if your use case is about finding opposition rather than estimating broad support.

Practical failure mode: High overall accuracy can hide poor detection of minority responses. For climate policies with broad support, the twin may be useful for estimating majority direction but risky for targeting skeptics or estimating the size of opposition.

Ordered response scales produced adjacent-category confusion

The skill-importance and social-media exercises used ordered scales. The models often confused adjacent or intensity-related categories:

  • “Extremely important” versus “Very important”
  • “Very well” versus “Somewhat well”
  • “Not too well” versus “Not at all well”

Those errors may be acceptable if the decision only needs broad direction. They are not acceptable if the decision depends on exact intensity.

In the skill-importance exercise, rare lower-importance responses were especially fragile:

  • One actual “Not too important” response was predicted as “Very important.”
  • One actual “Somewhat important” response was predicted as “Extremely important.”
  • One actual “Not at all important” response was predicted as “Not too important.”

google:gemini-2.5-pro had the most serious confidence failure here: it assigned effectively 0% probability to actual “Not too important” and “Somewhat important” answers in two misses.

Practical failure mode: If rare dissenting or low-intensity categories matter, do not rely on the top-line accuracy alone.

The AI medical-diagnosis exercise struggled with “about the same” and “not sure”

The AI question has a true uncertainty option and a midpoint-like option. Those are hard to predict:

  • An actual “AI would do this about the same” respondent was predicted as “AI would do this better” by both models in a recorded miss.
  • Actual “Not sure” respondents were sometimes predicted as “better” or “worse.”
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro was especially overconfident in some of these misses, including assigning only 2% probability to an actual “about the same” answer.

Practical failure mode: Treat “Not sure” and “about the same” as substantive choices, not as noise. If uncertainty or neutrality is important to the decision, validate.

The vignette exercise failed because the task was harder and the context was incomplete

The vignette exercise combines several known risk factors:

  • Seven ordered response options.
  • A blame-attribution judgment rather than a simple preference.
  • Experimental conditions summarized as labels, not full vignette prose.
  • Small evaluation sample of 20 respondents.
  • Responses spread across the agreement scale.

Both models failed to beat the group-average baseline on confidence quality. google:gemini-2.5-pro produced the most severe misses:

  • Actual “Strongly agree”, predicted “Strongly disagree,” with effectively 0% probability on the real answer.
  • Actual “Neither agree nor disagree,” predicted “Disagree,” with effectively 0% probability on the real answer.
  • Actual “Disagree,” predicted “Agree,” with only 1% probability on the real answer.

openai:gpt-5.5 avoided some of the most extreme probability failures but still did not beat the group-average baseline. Its top-choice accuracy was only 30%, the same as guessing from the group distribution.

Practical failure mode: Vignette twins need the full stimulus text. Summarized condition labels are not enough for reliable quantitative prediction.

google:gemini-2.5-pro had repeated overconfidence problems

google:gemini-2.5-pro sometimes achieved reasonable top-choice accuracy, but its probabilities were unreliable across multiple exercises:

  • Skill-importance: top-choice accuracy 70%, but two effectively zero-probability actual answers.
  • AI medical diagnosis: top-choice accuracy 50%, average top confidence 77.9%.
  • Social media: top-choice accuracy 55%, average top confidence 75.5%.
  • Vignette: top-choice accuracy 40%, but severe confidence failures and worse-than-random confidence-quality performance.
