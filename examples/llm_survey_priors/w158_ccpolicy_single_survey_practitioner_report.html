What This Report Means by Digital Twins

In this report, a digital twin is an AI simulation of an individual survey respondent: the model sees some of that respondent's prior survey answers, then predicts how the same respondent would answer a held-out or new question. One practical analogy is persona-based reasoning in product design and user research: practitioners have long used personas to make abstract customer data easier to reason with and to ask "what would this kind of person need, object to, or choose?" Digital twins are not the same as traditional personas, but they extend a related idea: they use many respondent-specific records, apply the persona-like reasoning at scale, and ask a model to project each respondent into a new decision setting.

This approach builds on a growing literature using language models to simulate human samples and behavior, including Argyle et al.'s work on "silicon samples" and algorithmic fidelity, Aher, Arriaga, and Kalai's "Turing Experiments" for replicating human-subject studies, Horton's framing of LLMs as simulated economic agents, and Park et al.'s generative-agent simulations of 1,000 real people. Expected Parrot is the company and open-source tooling provider behind EDSL, the system used here to build surveys, run language-model jobs remotely, store results, and generate this report.

References: Cooper, The Inmates Are Running the Asylum; Pruitt &amp; Grudin, Personas; Argyle et al., Out of One, Many; Aher, Arriaga &amp; Kalai, Using Large Language Models to Simulate Multiple Humans; Horton, Large Language Models as Simulated Economic Agents; Park et al., Generative Agent Simulations of 1,000 People; Expected Parrot EDSL documentation.

Why This Report Uses Held-Out Questions

The held-out question is a proxy for the kind of new question a practitioner might later ask an instantiated digital twin. In a deployed use case, the model would see what is already known about a respondent and then project that respondent into a new decision setting. The benchmark recreates that situation by hiding one real survey question, giving the twin other answers from the same respondent, and scoring whether the twin can recover the answer the respondent actually gave.

This is intentionally a demanding test. Survey designers usually avoid asking many highly correlated questions when they can, because redundant questions waste respondent attention. That means a held-out item is often not just a near-duplicate of the context questions. If a twin performs well under that condition, it is evidence that it learned something useful about the respondent or question family, not merely that it copied an obvious neighboring item.

The hold-out design is still only a proxy. It tests generalization to questions from the same survey environment, with known response options and a real answer for scoring. A truly new question may use different wording, a different scale, or a different decision context. Treat strong hold-out performance as evidence of promise, then adjust confidence based on how similar the new question is to the tested held-out questions.

How to Use This Report

Match Evidence to the Intended Use

Do not read one benchmark as a single yes/no answer about whether "digital twins work." A report may contain several twin exercises: different surveys, question families, held-out items, respondent samples, and models. Treat each exercise as evidence about a particular kind of use.

The standard of evidence depends less on whether the decision sounds "low" or "high" stakes in the abstract, and more on what the user needs the twin output to do:

  • Exact levels or public quantitative claims: require the strongest evidence. If the goal is to say "62% would choose A," publish a population estimate, set a hard cutoff, or target people based on predicted probabilities, inspect calibration and run stronger validation.
  • Rank ordering or prioritization: can tolerate more error in levels. If the goal is to decide which message, option, segment, or question is more promising, the key question is whether the benchmark shows reliable ordering, not whether every percentage is right.
  • Exploration and surfacing considerations: can be useful even when point estimates are uncertain. If the goal is to find likely objections, missing arguments, subgroup concerns, or hypotheses to investigate, the twin output should be judged as structured qualitative evidence rather than as a survey estimate.

Decision stakes still matter: the more public, irreversible, costly, or consequential the decision, the more direct validation is warranted. But a fast internal ranking exercise and a publishable estimate should not be held to the same standard.

Read Performance by Exercise

When a benchmark spans multiple surveys or held-out question families, discuss the results separately. A binary climate-policy exercise, a multi-option skill-importance exercise, a social-media attitude exercise, and a vignette blame exercise are not the same test. Strong performance in one exercise should not be generalized to all twins, and weak performance in one exercise should not erase evidence from another. Look for the pattern: which question types, option structures, and context signals worked, and which did not.

When Direct Survey Research Is Infeasible

Sometimes survey research is infeasible rather than merely expensive. Competitors, regulators, voters in a future election, executives, or other strategically important audiences may not answer a survey at all, may not answer quickly enough, or may not answer candidly. In those cases, the right comparison is not always "twin output versus a perfect survey"; it may be "twin output versus no direct measurement." Twins can be useful in that setting even when they are not precise enough to publish as population estimates.

Use Twins for Ranking and Exploration

Distinguish rank ordering from exact levels. Twins may be more reliable at identifying which option, message, segment, or question is relatively stronger than at estimating the exact percentage that would choose it.

They can also surface considerations, objections, and patterns to investigate, not just point-estimate predictions. For example, a twin study might suggest that a proposal is likely to worry respondents less because of its cost than because it feels unfair to a subgroup, or it might reveal an objection the research team had not thought to test directly. Treat those outputs as hypotheses and prompts for better thinking, not just as percentages.

Executive summary

The w158_ccpolicy climate-policy validation used a nationally representative Pew Research Center American Trends Panel survey of U.S. adults fielded in October 2024 to test survey-built twins on six held-out favor/oppose climate-change policy proposals. The validation covered 600 respondent-question cases per model — 100 cases for each of six questions — using openai:gpt-5.5 and google:gemini-2.5-pro, for 1,200 model-specific predictions in total. This survey’s twins are useful for new questions that look like the tested items: binary favor/oppose questions about concrete climate-policy proposals, with each respondent represented by answers to closely related climate-policy items.

The strongest practical result is that openai:gpt-5.5 is the better primary model for this survey’s twins. It picked the same answer as the real respondent in 83.8% of tested cases, compared with 50% for random guessing and 79.5% for guessing based only on how the whole group answered each held-out question. That means it was not just learning that most climate policies were popular; across the full validation, it added some respondent-specific signal. Its confidence was also broadly usable: when it was very confident, it was usually right, though not always.

Use the climate-policy twins most confidently for ranking and prioritizing similar binary policy proposals, especially when the proposal is not simply a near-consensus “Favor” item. The clearest evidence of respondent-specific value came on the more discriminating questions: taxing corporations based on carbon emissions and requiring power plants to eliminate all carbon emissions by 2040. On those items, the twins beat the group-average baseline by enough to suggest they were using the respondent’s other climate-policy answers in a useful way.

Be more cautious about exact quantitative estimates of support levels, especially for broadly popular proposals such as planting about a trillion trees, requiring oil and gas companies to seal methane leaks, and home energy-efficiency tax credits. On those items, much of the apparent performance came from the fact that “Favor” was common. For the tree-planting item, the group-average baseline actually outperformed both models on confidence quality, and outperformed both on simple accuracy for the tested sample.

Do not use google:gemini-2.5-pro alone for probability-sensitive decisions in this survey. It beat random guessing, but its overall accuracy, 78.8%, was slightly below the group-average baseline, 79.5%, and its confidence was uneven. In one large confidence band, it was about 83% confident on average but correct only 67% of the time. It can still be useful as a sensitivity check, especially on the power-plant mandate question, but it is weaker evidence for exact shares or cutoffs.

The main failure mode is important: the twins sometimes became very confident that a respondent would favor a policy because the respondent favored all or nearly all other climate policies, but the real respondent opposed the held-out policy. The worst misses were not random noise; they were systematic overconfidence around rare or idiosyncratic “Oppose” answers among otherwise pro-climate-policy respondents. If the intended use involves ranking respondents, setting probability thresholds, identifying persuadable segments, or making public quantitative claims, inspect these overconfident misses before relying on the numbers.

Study setup

Use this validation as evidence about new climate-policy favor/oppose questions that are close to the six tested questions. The survey source was a Pew Research Center American Trends Panel survey of U.S. adults, fielded in October 2024. The recorded survey contained 9,214 respondents, six climate-policy questions, and 55,284 answers. The validation used a sampled set of 100 respondent-question cases per held-out question per model.

The six held-out questions were all binary Favor/Oppose items:

  1. “Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate change? Planting about a trillion trees around the world to absorb carbon emissions in the atmosphere”
  2. “Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate change? Taxing corporations based on the amount of carbon emissions they produce”
  3. “Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate Providing a tax credit to encourage businesses to develop technology which captures and stores carbon emissions so they do not enter the atmosphere”
  4. “Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate change? CCPOLICY_d_W158. Requiring power plants to eliminate all carbon emissions by 2040”
  5. “Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate change? Requiring oil and gas companies to seal methane gas leaks from oil wells”
  6. “Do you favor or oppose below proposals to reduce effects of global climate change? Providing a tax credit to Americans who improve their home energy efficiency, such as by installing heat pumps or adding insulation”

Each held-out prediction used the respondent’s answers to the other climate-policy items as context. This is a strong setup for testing whether the twins can infer a respondent’s answer to a similar new climate-policy question, but it is a narrow setup: it does not validate multi-option climate questions, intensity scales, open-ended reasoning, tradeoff questions with explicit costs, or unrelated policy domains.

Two models were tested:

  • openai:gpt-5.5
  • google:gemini-2.5-pro

The recorded run imported 1,200 rows, extracted 1,200 rows, and reported zero import issues. The option labels were expanded to human-readable values, Favor and Oppose. The recorded artifacts do not state whether the validation metrics were weighted, so interpret the reported performance as performance on the tested validation cases rather than as weighted national estimates.

The report compares each model with two practical baselines:

  • Random guessing: with two options, this means choosing between Favor and Oppose with no information. Random guessing would assign the real answer a 50% chance.
  • Group-average guessing: this means guessing from how the whole tested group answered that held-out question. Beating this baseline matters because it shows the twin is using respondent-specific information, not just the overall popularity of the policy. This baseline is available for these held-out questions, but it would not be directly available for a genuinely new question whose real distribution has not been measured.

The practical takeaway is: use openai:gpt-5.5 as the main model for this survey’s twins; use google:gemini-2.5-pro mainly as a sensitivity check.

The openai model also assigned more probability to the answer the respondent actually gave: 79.1% on average, compared with 50.0% for random guessing and 68.8% for group-average guessing. That is useful evidence if the practitioner wants more than a top-line answer and plans to aggregate probabilities across respondents.

Confidence quality matters here because the models often gave high probabilities. The openai model’s average top-choice confidence was 87.3%, and it was correct 83.8% of the time. The google model’s average top-choice confidence was 88.3%, while accuracy was 78.8%. Its confidence was especially uneven in the 80–90% confidence range: average confidence was 83.4%, but accuracy was only 67.0% across 349 cases. Its highest confidence band performed well, but the middle-high confidence band is a red flag for cutoffs and rankings.

Where twins worked well

Use this survey’s twins for directional prediction and ranking among similar climate policies, especially when the policy is divisive enough that respondent-specific context matters.

The best evidence came from “Taxing corporations based on the amount of carbon emissions they produce”. In the tested cases, the group-average answer was not enough: group-average guessing was correct 70% of the time. The openai model reached 79%, and the google model reached 76%. This suggests the twins were using meaningful information from the respondent’s other climate-policy answers. The openai model was also fairly balanced on this item: it correctly identified 58 of 70 actual Favor responses and 21 of 30 actual Oppose responses.

The other strong item was “Requiring power plants to eliminate all carbon emissions by 2040.” This was the most divisive tested proposal, with group-average guessing correct only 62% of the time. Both models improved substantially: 79% for openai and 80% for google. This is the clearest sign that the twin setup can help when a new policy proposal is not just a broadly popular climate action. The google model was especially useful on actual Oppose answers for this question, correctly identifying 27 of 38 actual Oppose cases. OpenAI correctly identified 19 of 38 actual Oppose cases but was very strong on actual Favor, correctly identifying 60 of 62 actual Favor cases.

OpenAI also performed well on the two tax-credit items when judged as respondent-level prediction:

  • On the business carbon-capture tax credit question, openai accuracy was 85%, compared with 81% for group-average guessing.
  • On the home energy-efficiency tax credit question, openai accuracy was 88%, compared with 86% for group-average guessing.

These are small gains over the group-average baseline in simple accuracy, but the pattern supports using the openai model for similar favor/oppose climate-policy questions when the intended use is ranking proposals or estimating broad direction.

The likely reason these items worked is that the six questions share a common policy domain and a simple answer structure. Respondents’ other answers reveal whether they generally favor climate mitigation, whether they accept corporate regulation or costs, and whether they distinguish incentives from mandates. The twins could use those patterns. For example, a respondent who favored carbon taxes, methane leak requirements, and tax credits was usually likely to favor another climate mitigation proposal.

For internal prioritization, message screening, or deciding which policy concepts deserve follow-up research, the openai results are strong enough to act on when the new question is close to these tested items. For public claims, published estimates, or decisions where a few percentage points change the conclusion, the current validation is not enough by itself because each question-level estimate is based on only 100 tested cases per model and the metrics are not documented as weighted.

Where twins failed

The main failure mode was very confident support predictions for respondents who actually opposed the held-out policy. This matters because the tested policy area contains many majority-Favor items. A model can look strong overall while still missing the minority response that a practitioner may care most about.

The clearest example was the tree-planting question:

“Do you favor or oppose the following proposals to reduce the effects of global climate change? Planting about a trillion trees around the world to absorb carbon emissions in the atmosphere”

This was a broadly popular item in the tested cases. OpenAI achieved 89% accuracy, but the group-average baseline achieved 91%. In other words, for this item, knowing the overall popularity of the proposal was at least as useful as the twin’s respondent-specific inference.

The worst openai misses on this item were severe. In multiple cases, the respondent favored every observed climate-change mitigation proposal in the context — including taxes, regulations, methane leak requirements, and tax credits — but actually answered Oppose to tree planting. The openai model assigned only 1% or 2% probability to the actual Oppose answer. The google model made similar misses, assigning 5% probability to the actual Oppose answer in several of the same pattern cases.

This failure is understandable but important: the models inferred that a consistent pro-climate-policy pattern implied near-certain support for tree planting. That inference is usually right, but not always. The validation shows that some respondents have idiosyncratic or proposal-specific opposition that is not captured by their answers to the other five policy items.

The same pattern appeared for the home energy-efficiency tax credit:

“Do you favor or oppose below proposals to reduce effects of global climate change? Providing a tax credit to Americans who improve their home energy efficiency, such as by installing heat pumps or adding insulation”

OpenAI reached 88% accuracy, but some of its worst misses assigned 98% probability to Favor when the respondent actually answered Oppose. These respondents often favored every other observed policy, including another tax-credit proposal and regulatory requirements. The model treated that pattern as near-certain support for the home energy tax credit, but the real answer showed that support for climate policy generally did not guarantee support for every incentive design.

The methane leak requirement showed a related issue:

“Requiring oil and gas companies to seal methane gas leaks from oil wells”

The group-average baseline was correct 87% of the time, while openai was correct 83% and google 80%. OpenAI did identify some actual Oppose respondents — 8 of 13 — but at the cost of incorrectly predicting Oppose for 12 actual Favor respondents. For estimating the top-line answer to this specific item, the group average would have been better. For identifying possible minority opposition, the twin signal may still be informative, but it needs review because the false positives are substantial in this small sample.

The google model had an additional failure mode: it often produced high confidence even when accuracy did not justify it. Across 349 cases in its 80–90% confidence band, it was correct only 67.0% of the time. That makes google risky for probability thresholds such as “show me respondents with at least 85% probability of favoring this proposal.”

The validation also shows that the twins are not equally good at both sides of the binary choice. On popular items, the models were often better at identifying Favor than Oppose. For example:

  • On the tree-planting question, openai correctly predicted 89 of 91 actual Favor responses, but missed all 9 actual Oppose responses.
  • On the home energy-efficiency tax credit, openai correctly predicted 85 of 86 actual Favor responses, but only 3 of 14 actual Oppose responses.
  • On the business carbon-capture tax credit, openai correctly predicted 76 of 81 actual Favor responses and 9 of 19 actual Oppose responses.

This does not mean the twins are unusable. It means the practitioner should be careful when the decision depends on identifying opposition among people who otherwise look supportive.

Practical use recommendations

For exact quantitative estimates, use the openai version of this survey’s twins only when the new question is very close to the tested format: a binary Favor/Oppose climate-policy proposal asked of U.S. adults, with respondent context from nearby climate-policy questions. The current validation supports internal estimates and directional planning, but it does not by itself support publication-grade national percentages. The artifacts do not indicate weighting, and each held-out question was tested on 100 cases per model, so sampling variation at the question level is meaningful.

The strongest exact-estimate candidates are new questions resembling the more discriminating tested policies, such as corporate emissions costs or stringent emissions requirements. The power-plant mandate result is especially encouraging because both models beat group-average guessing by a large margin. If a new question asks about a similarly concrete and potentially divisive climate policy, openai twin estimates are worth using as decision evidence, with google as a sensitivity check.

For broad-consensus proposals, treat exact levels more cautiously. The tree-planting and methane-leak items show that high apparent performance can come from majority support rather than from individual-level inference. If a new proposal is expected to be overwhelmingly favored, the twin may correctly rank it highly but still overstate certainty and under-detect the minority who would oppose it.

For ranking and prioritization, the evidence is stronger. The openai model’s overall performance, its gains over group-average guessing on several questions, and its generally reasonable confidence quality make it useful for ranking similar climate-policy proposals for internal planning. A good use would be: compare several new policy wordings, identify which ones appear relatively stronger or more divisive, and choose a smaller set for field testing or stakeholder review.

When using twins for ranking, do not rely only on average predicted support. Also inspect:

  • whether the ranking is driven by one model or appears across both models;
  • whether the top-ranked proposal is simply a generic, broadly popular climate action;
  • whether the model is assigning extreme probabilities to nearly everyone;
  • whether predicted opposition is concentrated among respondents with consistent opposition patterns, or whether it is detecting plausible mechanism-specific breaks.

For exploration and surfacing considerations, the twins are useful but should be read as structured hypotheses, not as direct evidence of respondents’ reasons. The recorded responses are binary, so the validation does not contain real open-ended explanations. Still, the failure cases surface important assumptions worth testing:

  • The models assume that support for one climate mitigation proposal often generalizes to support for another.
  • They distinguish policy mechanisms such as taxes, regulation, and tax credits, but sometimes treat a consistent pro-climate pattern as near-certainty.
  • They can miss respondents who favor many climate policies but oppose a specific design, target, or implementation detail.
  • They may treat tree planting and tax credits as broadly acceptable even when a minority of respondents reject them.

Practitioners can use those patterns to improve follow-up research. For example, if a new proposal is a tax credit, add validation or review around people who support climate action generally but may object to subsidies, eligibility, consumer burden, or implementation design. If a proposal is a strict mandate, compare it with the power-plant 2040 item, where respondent-specific signal was strong but some otherwise supportive respondents still opposed the mandate.

For probability cutoffs or targeting, inspect confidence failures before acting. The openai model was well calibrated overall, but its worst misses assigned only 1–4% probability to the answer the respondent actually gave. The google model had a larger confidence-quality problem in its 80–90% confidence range. If the intended decision is “target everyone above 80% predicted support” or “exclude everyone below 20% predicted opposition,” run a miss review first and consider capping extreme probabilities so that a 98–99% model probability is not treated as certainty.

For model choice, prefer this ordering:

  1. Primary: openai:gpt-5.5 for aggregate estimates, ranking, and respondent-level simulation in this specific survey domain.
  2. Secondary check: google:gemini-2.5-pro to see whether conclusions are robust, especially for divisive proposals like the power-plant mandate.
  3. Do not use google alone for exact estimates or probability thresholds unless additional validation shows better calibration for the planned question type.

For new-question extrapolation, stay close to the validation domain. This evidence is most relevant for questions that are:

  • about climate-change mitigation policy;
  • phrased as Favor/Oppose;
  • concrete rather than abstract;
  • similar in complexity to the six tested items;
  • asked of a U.S. adult population similar to the Pew ATP survey context;
  • interpreted using respondent context from nearby climate-policy items.

It is much weaker evidence for questions about climate beliefs, partisan identity, willingness to pay, candidate choice, open-ended messaging, multi-option policy bundles, or detailed cost-benefit tradeoffs.

Next study recommendations

For a quick sanity check before using this survey’s twins on a new climate-policy question, run the new item through both tested models and compare the aggregate results. If openai and google disagree materially, treat the result as directional only. Also inspect a sample of high-confidence predictions, especially cases where the model predicts near-certain Favor or near-certain Oppose.

For a low-cost improvement, add a simple overconfidence review. Pull the cases with the most extreme predicted probabilities and ask whether the respondent context truly justifies that certainty. The current validation shows that the riskiest errors often occur when a respondent favored all or nearly all other climate policies but opposed the held-out one. That is the first pattern to check in future runs.

For decisions that need better exact estimates, expand the held-out validation. Six questions is useful, but it is still a narrow set. Add more climate-policy items with varied policy mechanisms:

  • subsidies and tax credits;
  • corporate taxes and fees;
  • mandates with different deadlines;
  • household-level requirements;
  • infrastructure or land-use policies;
  • policies with explicit costs;
  • policies that are expected to have closer support/opposition splits.

For public, expensive, or hard-to-reverse decisions, validate with a larger respondent sample per question. The current 100 cases per question per model are enough to reveal broad patterns and failure modes, but not enough to make fine-grained claims about a few percentage-point differences.

For probability-sensitive uses, run a calibration check on the specific planned setup. OpenAI’s overall confidence quality was good in this validation, but the worst misses were severe. Gemini’s confidence quality was weaker. If the output will feed thresholds, rankings, or targeting, calibrate or dampen probabilities before using them operationally.

For subgroup decisions, add subgroup validation. The recorded artifacts do not provide subgroup performance by party, ideology, education, geography, age, or climate attitudes. If the practitioner wants to use twins to compare segments, validate segment-level accuracy and confidence quality directly rather than assuming the overall 83.8% openai accuracy applies equally across groups.

For better model robustness, keep comparing models and prompts. The two tested models behaved differently: openai was better overall, while google was competitive on the power-plant mandate but weaker on several popular or incentive items. A future study should test whether a prompt that explicitly discourages extreme certainty reduces the worst overconfident misses without sacrificing too much accuracy.

The highest-value next validation is therefore: more held-out climate-policy questions, larger per-question samples, weighted performance checks if national estimates are the goal, and a focused review of rare Oppose answers among otherwise pro-climate-policy respondents.
